👻 GHOST
Generalizing Skills By Hierarchical Learning from Human Demonstrations

Abstract.
We present GHOST, a framework for generalizing robot skills to new tasks through human demonstrations. Robot teleoperation data is critical for training robust policies but is expensive to scale, while human video is abundant but lacks action labels.

We use a hierarchical policy framework that decouples a policy into two parts - The high-level policy maps visual observations to task "sub-goals". These sub-goals are intermediate objectives like "grasp the handle" defined in a task-relevant but embodiment-agnostic manner, and does not require action labels. The low-level policy is conditioned on these goals to execute embodiment-specific actions, and is trained purely on robot data. This setup provides two key benefits. First, even when trained purely on teleoperation data, it significantly boosts performance. Second, it enables out-of-distribution generalization through human demonstrations - allowing us to learn precise embodiment-specific skills from robot data and then generalize these learned skills across novel objects and compositions of these skills.

We demonstrate the results from our framework across multiple challenging real-world tasks including pick-and-place, cloth folding, and manipulation of articulated objects.
Approach.

System Overview Figure Placeholder

GHOST Framework Overview: The hierarchical policy consists of a high-level goal prediction network that predicts end-effector sub-goals and a low-level goal-conditioned policy that executes actions to reach those goals.

GHOST decomposes the policy learning problem into two components: a high-level policy that predicts spatial sub-goals in a task-specific but embodiment-agnostic manner, and a low-level policy that learns embodiment-specific skills to achieve those goals. This factorization enables the high-level policy to be trained on both human and robot data, while the low-level policy is trained exclusively on robot demonstrations to learn precise manipulation behaviors.
1. Data Collection and Processing.
We collect two types of demonstrations: robot teleoperation data and human demonstrations. For robot data, we collect demonstrations across multiple task variants, each demonstrating the same skill (e.g., pick-and-place), to provide in-distribution coverage. Sub-goals are extracted automatically by identifying timesteps where the gripper state changes. For human data, we collect demonstrations of the same skill in novel settings using new objects or novel applications. We track 3D hand poses using off-the-shelf hand pose estimators and manually annotate sub-goal timesteps.

To enable cross-embodiment transfer, we represent end-effector poses as a set of 4 3D keypoints that can be extracted from both robot grippers and human hands: the gripper base/palm, finger tips, and grasping center. This unified representation allows the high-level policy to be trained on both robot and human demonstrations.
2. High-Level Goal Prediction Policy.

High-Level Goal Prediction Architecture Placeholder

High-Level Policy Architecture: An encoder-only transformer processes RGB-D observations from multiple cameras with frozen DINOv2 features. Each patch token predicts mixing weights and 3D residual vectors for a Gaussian Mixture Model representing the distribution over sub-goal end-effector poses.

The high-level policy predicts the end-effector pose at the next sub-goal timestep. We parameterize it as an encoder-only transformer operating on RGB-D observations from multiple cameras. Each RGB-D image is processed with a frozen DINOv2 encoder, producing patch tokens that are augmented with 3D coordinates derived from depth. To handle multimodality in demonstrations, we model the goal distribution as a per-patch Gaussian Mixture Model where each patch predicts mixing weights and 3D residual vectors relative to the patch center.
3. Low-Level Goal-Conditioned Policy.

Low-Level Goal-Conditioned Policy Architecture Placeholder

Low-Level Policy Architecture: A Diffusion Policy that generates action chunks conditioned on observations and end-effector heatmaps. The 3D goals are projected to image planes and represented as dense heatmaps encoding pixel distance fields.

We instantiate the low-level policy as a Diffusion Policy that generates chunks of actions by denoising conditioned on observations and spatial goals. The 3D goals predicted by the high-level policy are projected to each camera's image plane and converted to dense end-effector heatmaps. These heatmaps encode the pixel distance field from each keypoint, providing a spatial representation that preserves structure while densifying the sparse goal information. This approach allows the policy to effectively condition on goals while leveraging standard image-based architectures.

Cross-Embodiment End-Effector Representation: We represent end-effector poses as a set of 4 3D keypoints (gripper base, finger tips, grasping center) that can be extracted from both robot grippers and human hands, enabling a unified goal representation across embodiments.

Experiments and Results.
We evaluate GHOST across multiple challenging real-world manipulation tasks to demonstrate both in-distribution performance improvements and out-of-distribution generalization through human demonstrations. Our experiments span pick-and-place, cloth folding, and articulated object manipulation tasks.
